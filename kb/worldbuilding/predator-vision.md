Q:

What is known or theorized about the vision of dinosaurs? What is known about the vision of crocodiles or snakes? Do they see in color? Do they see into the infrared, sensing heat?

A:

Great questions! Let me break down what we know about vision in these different reptilian groups.

## Dinosaur Vision

Dinosaurs likely had **excellent color vision**, potentially even better than humans. This is based on several lines of evidence:

- **Living relatives**: Both birds (direct dinosaur descendants) and crocodilians (their closest living relatives) have tetrachromatic vision - they see four color channels compared to our three, allowing them to perceive colors in the ultraviolet range we can't see.

- **Skull anatomy**: Fossil skulls preserve eye socket sizes and shapes. Large eye sockets relative to skull size (like in *Tyrannosaurus rex*) suggest excellent vision, including potentially good depth perception from forward-facing eyes.

- **Scleral rings**: These are bony rings in the eye preserved in many dinosaur fossils that tell us about eye shape - whether adapted for day or night vision, and activity patterns.

- **Ecological niches**: Predatory dinosaurs especially would have benefited from sharp color vision for hunting, while herbivores would have needed it to distinguish edible plants.

Different dinosaurs likely had different visual capabilities based on their lifestyle - nocturnal species may have sacrificed color vision for better night vision, similar to modern animals.

## Crocodile Vision

Crocodiles have surprisingly sophisticated vision:

- **Color vision**: Yes! They have color vision, though it's most acute in good lighting. Their retinas contain both rods (for low light) and cones (for color).

- **Adaptations**: They have vertical slit pupils that can close almost completely, protecting their eyes in bright light. They also have a tapetum lucidum (reflective layer) that makes their eyes glow at night and improves night vision.

- **No infrared**: Crocodiles do **not** see in infrared or sense heat with their eyes. However, they have highly sensitive temperature-detecting organs on their jaws (called integumentary sense organs) that help them detect prey in water.

## Snake Vision

Snake vision is **highly variable** depending on species:

- **Color vision**: Many snakes do see in color, but it depends on the species. Diurnal (day-active) snakes often have good color vision, while nocturnal species may see primarily in black and white.

- **Infrared "vision"**: This is where it gets fascinating! Some snakes - notably pit vipers (rattlesnakes, copperheads), pythons, and boas - have specialized **pit organs** that detect infrared radiation (heat). These aren't eyes and don't produce "images" like vision, but rather create a heat map overlay that helps them hunt warm-blooded prey in complete darkness. The pits are located between the eye and nostril in pit vipers, or along the lips in pythons and boas.

- **UV vision**: Some snakes can see into the ultraviolet range, which may help them track prey trails.
